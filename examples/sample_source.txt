The rain had a way of making the city sound older than it was. By midnight the lamps on Mercer Street
were halos in the mist, and every passerby looked like someone with a secret to keep. Evelyn had been
waiting outside the telegraph office for nearly an hour when the final message came through. It was brief,
unsigned, and impossible to misunderstand: do not trust the man with the silver watch.

She read it twice before folding the paper into her glove. Across the street, a taxi idled under the awning
of the Astor Hotel. The driver never looked up, but the ember of his cigarette glowed like a warning.
Inspector Hale emerged from the shadows a moment later, carrying the fatigue of three sleepless nights and
the certainty that the case had turned against him. Somewhere in the harbor a ship horn sounded, low and mournful.

There were too many names, too many lies, and one missing ledger that everyone in the city seemed prepared
to kill for. Evelyn thought of her brother, of the promise she had made to keep him clear of scandal, and of
the locked office on the sixth floor where the truth might already be burning in a wastebasket.
